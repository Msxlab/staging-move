# Trust And Safety Experience

## Current verified experience

- Public "how it works" copy says LocateFlow organizes providers, reminders, and tasks without replacing provider confirmation.
- Service creation copy states that adding a service creates a LocateFlow record and does not update the external provider account.
- Provider recommendation copy is framed as listed providers and manual tracking.
- AI briefing generation is constrained to avoid legal, tax, financial advice and avoid invented dates or requirements.
- Vehicle/move safety surfaces link users to official NHTSA and DMV resources where appropriate.
- Home Dossier sections include visible disclaimers and source-backed limitations.
- Export flows use authentication, rate limiting, step-up verification, and sensitive-field masking.

## User goal

Know exactly what LocateFlow can do, what it cannot do, and when a provider or official source must be checked directly.

## Emotional job

Feel protected rather than misled while making high-consequence address, provider, billing, and move decisions.

## Current friction

- Trust copy exists, but it is distributed across public, service, AI, dossier, vehicle, and export surfaces.
- Provider Transition could be misunderstood as automatic account-change execution unless copy stays explicit.
- AI Move Briefing needs source and limitation framing wherever it becomes a monetized experience.
- Proof packets need clear privacy, scope, and "not official certification" boundaries.

## Aha opportunity

"LocateFlow is honest about what it tracks, what it recommends, and what still requires my confirmation."

The strongest trust aha is a consistent product language system: records, reminders, recommendations, proof packets, and official-source checks.

## Revenue opportunity

- Trustworthy limits can increase paid conversion because users understand the value without fearing overreach.
- Pro proof packets can monetize confidence if they avoid official-certification claims.
- AI Briefing can monetize guidance if it stays evidence-bound and reversible.

## Data moat opportunity

- Consented, coarse signals: confirmation method, proof generated, official-source link clicked, blocker category, task status.
- Avoid collecting raw provider credentials, account numbers, confirmation codes, documents, raw addresses, or sensitive user notes for moat purposes.

## Hypotheses

- Hypothesis: explicit "we do not automatically change your provider account" copy reduces support risk and increases trust.
- Hypothesis: source/limitation explanations improve AI Briefing acceptance.
- Hypothesis: users will pay for trustworthy organization and proof, even without direct provider automation.

## Recommended experiments

1. Trust-language audit across AI Briefing, services, Provider Transition, exports, and pricing.
2. AI Briefing source and limitation explainer near generated recommendations.
3. Proof-packet privacy preview before export generation.

## Possible Codex implementation tasks

- Create a docs-only trust copy rulebook for AI, provider, export, billing, and rules surfaces.
- Draft a source-change task plan for AI Briefing source/limitation UI, then stop for approval.
- Draft privacy QA checks for analytics, exports, and proof-packet events.
